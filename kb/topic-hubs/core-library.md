A modern framing of Software 1.0, 2.0, and 3.0, arguing that natural language and LLMs are reshaping how software gets authored and operated.

The strongest single-volume history of venture capital in the United States, from early risk finance to modern Silicon Valley.

### [Neural Networks (video series)](../cards/3blue1brown-neural-networks.md)

A beautifully animated video series that builds visual intuition for how neural networks learn, what gradient descent does, and how backpropagation works, all from first principles.

A modern update to the software craft classic focused on practical, professional programming habits.

### [The Innovator's Dilemma](../cards/the-innovator-s-dilemma.md)
*Clayton M. Christensen · 1997 · Book · Startup History*

Explains how successful companies can fail when new technologies start in low-end or overlooked markets.

### [The Bitter Lesson](../cards/the-bitter-lesson.md)
*Richard Sutton · 2019 · Essay · AI Foundations*

A short, influential essay arguing that across 70 years of AI research, general methods that leverage computation (search and learning) consistently beat approaches that build in human knowledge. The 'bitter' part is how often researchers resist this.

### [Software 2.0](../cards/software-2-0.md)
*Andrej Karpathy · 2017 · Essay · AI in Practice*

An essay reframing neural networks as a new kind of software: instead of writing explicit code, you curate datasets and let optimization write the program in weight-space. It reshapes how to think about building with ML.

### [The Hard Thing About Hard Things](../cards/hard-thing-about-hard-things.md)
*Ben Horowitz · 2014 · Book · Startup History*

A blunt, experience-driven book on the brutal parts of running a company that no one prepares you for: layoffs, near-death moments, demoting friends, and making decisions with no good options.

### [How to Start a Startup (Stanford CS183B)](../cards/how-to-start-a-startup-cs183b.md)
*Sam Altman, Y Combinator, et al. · 2014 · Lecture Series · Startup History*

A free Stanford lecture series organized by Y Combinator, with talks from Sam Altman, Paul Graham, Peter Thiel, Marc Andreessen, and others covering ideas, teams, product, growth, and fundraising.

### [Do Things That Don't Scale](../cards/do-things-that-dont-scale.md)
*Paul Graham · 2013 · Essay · Founder Mental Models*

A Paul Graham essay arguing that early-stage founders should do manual, unscalable things — personally recruiting users, delivering delight by hand — because that's how startups actually get their first traction.

### [How to Do Great Work](../cards/how-to-do-great-work.md)
*Paul Graham · 2023 · Essay · Founder Mental Models*

A long essay distilling what people who do great work tend to have in common: working on things you're genuinely curious about, the discipline to follow that curiosity, and the courage to work on ambitious problems.

### [You and Your Research](../cards/hamming-you-and-your-research.md)
*Richard Hamming · 1986 · Talk · Founder Mental Models*

A famous Bell Labs talk on why some scientists do Nobel-caliber work and others don't. Hamming argues it's less about raw genius and more about courage, working on important problems, and managing yourself.

*Don Norman · 1988 · Book · Product and Design*

The foundational text of user-centered design, introducing affordances, signifiers, mapping, and feedback. It teaches you to blame bad design, not users, and to see the designed world everywhere.

A detailed Paul Graham essay on the mechanics and psychology of raising a seed round: getting to a strong position first, talking to investors in parallel, managing momentum, and not letting fundraising consume the company.

Turing's seminal essay asking 'Can machines think?' and proposing the Imitation Game (the Turing Test) as an operational substitute for the question. It anticipated and rebutted most objections to machine intelligence still debated today.

A contrarian set of essays on building valuable companies, arguing that great startups create something new ('zero to one') rather than copying ('one to n'), and that monopolies built on real differentiation drive progress.

### [Inventing on Principle](../cards/inventing-on-principle.md)
*Bret Victor · 2012 · Talk · Product and Design*

A landmark talk demonstrating live, immediate feedback between creators and their creations, and arguing that the most meaningful work comes from following a guiding principle. Visually stunning and intellectually provocative.

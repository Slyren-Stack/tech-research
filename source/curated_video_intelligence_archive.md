# Curated Video Intelligence Archive

A ranked library of foundational, high-signal watchable knowledge for founders, builders, designers, and technical teams.

## What belongs in the core library

The core stack is the shortest path to practical understanding: startup fundamentals, customer discovery, ML/deep-learning foundations, AI-native software thinking, design thinking, product craft, delivery discipline, and computer/internet history.

## Core watchlist (ranked)

1. **Lecture 1 - How to Start a Startup** — Sam Altman, Dustin Moskovitz (2014) — 43:53.

   - The canonical startup orientation: idea, product, team, execution, and why founders need speed, clarity, and resilience.

   - Tags: startups, YC, founder basics, execution, team

2. **Before the Startup** — Paul Graham (2014) — 48:21.

## What is historically important but older / legacy

The Mother of All Demos, Steve Jobs' Macintosh unveiling, Steve Jobs: The Lost Interview, and Computer Chronicles are legacy recordings: they are outdated as operational playbooks, but essential for understanding how modern product culture, GUI interaction, and the web emerged.


## What is still missing from this corpus

Security, privacy, compilers, databases, distributed systems at scale, mobile product history, cloud infrastructure, hardware, accessibility, B2B sales, recruiting, pricing, growth loops, open-source governance, and non-US startup ecosystems need additional coverage.

### Advanced founder / builder path

Lecture 2: Team and Execution → The Hard Thing About Hard Things → Marty Cagan → Continuous Discovery Habits → CS229 → Software Is Changing (Again) → OpenAI DevDay.

### Design / product path

Tim Brown → IDEO U Design Thinking → Don Norman → Marty Cagan → Teresa Torres → product execution and experimentation loops.

### Technical path

CS229 → CS231n Backprop → CS224N → micrograd → GPT from scratch → Software Is Changing (Again) → Continuous Delivery.


## Biggest lessons

1. Startups are experiments under uncertainty.
2. Customers reveal the truth through behavior, not opinions.
3. Great products are built through hand-to-hand craft before scale.
4. AI changes the authoring model of software, not the need for taste, product judgment, or engineering rigor.
5. The best teams combine technical depth with strong operating habits and a sharp sense of history.
